**1. On the On-path / Off-path Fault Line**
The memo suggests using the statement "equilibrium concepts are statements about off-path behavior, whereas learning systems only observe on-path data" as the unifying refrain of the paper. The first half is valid; however, the second half directly conflicts with the core methods reviewed in this paper. CFR traverses information sets unreachable by the current strategy in each round and computes counterfactual values for them (Zinkevich et al., 2007); RegretNet's training inner loop performs an explicit search over the misreport space, and ex-post regret is the optimized quantity rather than an unobservable one (Dütting et al., 2019); PSRO's best-response oracle is inherently a deviation searcher (Lanctot et al., 2017). The defining action of these methods is precisely querying off-path objects. If we establish "learning only sees on-path data" as the refrain, most of the materials in Sections 3.1 and 3.3 would serve as counterexamples. Furthermore, "on/off-path" is lexically close to the reinforcement learning terms "on/off-policy," making it easy for readers from a multi-agent background to conflate the two.

We will retain the memo's intent (a technical thread running through the six subsections) and revise the formulation to: Certificates perform universal quantification over the counterfactual space ($\forall$ deviations, $\forall$ types, $\forall$ opponents), while any computational process has only finite access to this space. Finite access falls into two regimes. Under the *query regime*, counterfactuals can be computed at a cost via a simulator; the obstacle here is computational—the space is too large to exhaust. Sections 3.1 and 3.3 instantiate the refrain within this regime. Under the *observational regime*, there is only empirical behavioral data, and counterfactuals cannot be queried; the obstacle is epistemological. Sections 3.2 and 4.3 instantiate this. Certifying universal propositions from finite access constitutes a third obstacle, which is addressed by debate in Section 4.2 and auditing in Section 4.3. This revision aligns with the existing three-obstacle paragraph in the introduction, effectively elevating the draft's existing taxonomy to the refrain. The "on/off-path" language will be reserved for the observational regime, where it remains accurate.

**2. On Verifiable Strategic Certificates**
Agreed, and we plan to use this as the load-bearing claim of the paper. Before adopting it, we need to supply a definition, otherwise it remains just a label: the objects subsumed under this name include symbolic proofs (PrimeNash), construct-by-design structural guarantees (the menu/AMA family, GemNet), protocol-level guarantees (the polynomial-time winning strategy of the honest party in doubly-efficient debate), and behavioral statistical tests (calibrated regret). These four differ in their quantified objects, white-box vs. black-box nature, and trust models. The unifying property is the decoupling of verification cost from construction cost: construction is hard, but verification, once given, is easy. This property is isomorphic to the PPAD narrative already laid out in Section 2.1 (solutions are guaranteed to exist but hard to find, yet easy to verify given), thereby connecting the definition to a historical arc rather than naming it out of thin air.

*Placement:* Section 5 will provide the naming, definition, and a small taxonomy table (Certificate Type × Quantified Object × Verification Method × Trust Model); the three-node loop of *specification → construction → verification* will also be written into the forward-referencing sentence of Section 1, step (5). Research directions 1 and 6 will be merged under this claim, per the anticipation in your memo.

**3. On LLM Agents as the Collapse Point**
The memo claims that the LLM agent is "the first entity to simultaneously act as the constructor of the strategic specification and the strategic subject," with the criterion being its presence in all six quadrants. We have two reservations. First, self-play systems have long simultaneously constructed equilibria and acted as players within them (AlphaZero, Silver et al., 2018); dual-sided presence was not pioneered by LLMs. "Deep learning" as a methodology family is also trivially present in all six quadrants, so the "six-quadrant presence" criterion fails to isolate LLMs. Second, this proposition does not answer *why* LLMs are special; existing "LLM meets game theory" reviews have already laid out the inventory of LLMs' presence, and singling them out here might inadvertently tie the paper to a single generation of artifacts, contradicting our goal of longevity.

*Revision:* We will narrow the proposition using the paper's own coordinate system. Previous learning systems, even when constructing equilibria, output numerical strategies and thus always remained on the *construction* side; LLMs are the first class of learning systems to simultaneously produce and consume objects on the *specification* side: proofs (PrimeNash), mechanism-as-code (LLM-AMD), and interpretable symbolic behavioral models (AutoBM). The reason for this can be pinpointed: they operate within the symbolic medium where specifications are written. To avoid making intensional assertions about the "essential capabilities of AI" that lack academic consensus, we will not introduce a complete capability taxonomy layer. Instead, we will place an extensional table in Section 5, reading out the capabilities that actually bear the load in each quadrant (gradient residualization, no-regret dynamics, population search, symbolic generation); the origins of these capabilities in each row have already been pre-established via historical narrative in the five movements of Section 2.2. The table simply reads them out and does not constitute a new theory of AI. The LLM observation is thus presented as a row pattern in the table: the quadrants where certificates are fully recovered are exactly those where symbolic generation capabilities enter. The main text will use artifact-agnostic phrasing, such as "learning systems operating within the writing medium of specifications."

**4. On the Fourth Turn of Game Theory**
This point and Point 2 are two registers of the same claim: Point 2 names the object, and Point 4 reads the appearance of the object as disciplinary history. Whether the Fourth Turn holds depends entirely on the evidence of certificates, so we plan to treat it as a corollary of the certificate claim, adding one sentence each to the abstract, Section 1, and the final paragraph of Section 5, rather than listing it as a separate, parallel point. Parallel statements would feel redundant to readers. Additionally, a distinction needs to be made. The Third Turn (AGT) inherently contains a verification aspect; PPAD is precisely about solutions being hard to find but easy to verify given. If we do not articulate the increment, readers from a complexity background might collapse the Fourth Turn back into the Third Turn. The increment is this: the object being certified has shifted from an explicitly given game to a deployed learning system. The game itself can only be estimated empirically, and verification must proceed from structure or behavior, rather than from the description of the game. This distinction will be seeded at the end of Section 2.1 and explicitly stated in Section 5.

**5. Defensive Sentence on Equilibrium and Robustness**
We agree this sentence is needed, and it must provide defense in both directions. We adopt the "left stake" provided in the memo (the robustness of an equilibrium must be reciprocal and hold simultaneously among all parties, distinct from one-sided minimax against nature). Setting only the left stake, however, would push readers toward another misinterpretation: treating equilibrium as a stable resting point in a dynamical sense. Equilibrium is a fixed point of the best-response mapping, a purely static consistency condition that carries no claims of convergence or stability; the box in Section 4.2 already includes results on the separation of stable fixed points and local Nash (Balduzzi et al., 2018) and the last iterate rotating around Nash (Mertikopoulos et al., 2018), so the "right stake" can be nailed down using the paper's own corpus. The same logic applies to the left stake: the zero-sum case is precisely the special case where equilibrium degenerates into minimax robustness. This explains the source of the confusion and serves as a reminder that a large number of the solvers in this paper focus on the zero-sum domain, objectively partially enabling this misreading, making it all the more necessary to point it out.

*Placement:* One sentence in Section 1; fully expanded in Section 2.1, step (2), with a forward hook to the box in Section 4.2. With this treatment, the defensive sentence also takes on an explanatory function: the separation of consistency and stability is exactly the reason for the rotational and failure dynamics of training in Section 4.2.
