# Introduction draft

## Writing logic

This draft tightens the introduction around one organizing claim:

> The meeting point between AI and game theory is not simply that each can be used by the other. It is that both fields now face the same counterfactual problem from opposite sides. Game theory defines strategic properties by quantifying over deviations, beliefs, types, opponents, and institutions that are not directly observed. AI supplies scalable procedures for search, learning, and simulation, but those procedures need game-theoretic concepts to become evaluable, trainable, and governable under interaction.

The core line is therefore:

```text
Strategic interaction is becoming computational.

Game theory -> precise counterfactual specifications,
but hard to construct, infer, and implement at scale.

AI -> scalable procedures that can search, simulate, and act,
but hard to evaluate, align, and govern once other agents adapt.

The bridge:
AI for game theory = making strategic specifications executable.
Game theory for AI = making AI behavior relationally robust.
```

The introduction below avoids making "specification vs. execution" the only abstraction. Instead, it names the concrete gaps that Sections 3 and 4 actually study: construction, inference, incentive embedding, evaluation, training, and deployment governance.

## Draft

\section{Introduction}

Game theory was built to reason about situations in which outcomes depend not only on what one agent does, but on how other agents respond. Its central concepts---equilibrium, belief, incentive compatibility, rationalizability, common knowledge, and mechanism design---are all ways of disciplining this dependence. They do not merely describe observed behavior. They ask whether a proposed outcome would survive deviations, whether beliefs could have generated the observed play, whether truthful reporting would remain optimal under all possible misreports, or whether an institution would still perform well once participants understand and adapt to its rules. In this sense, game theory is a language for counterfactual robustness in interactive systems.

That language is more relevant than ever because artificial intelligence is becoming interactive in precisely this sense. Modern AI systems are no longer only predictors trained on fixed datasets. Reinforcement-learning agents learn through repeated feedback; self-play systems improve against changing opponents; language-model agents negotiate, persuade, delegate, and coordinate; pricing, recommendation, and trading algorithms increasingly operate in environments shaped by other algorithms and human responses. Once AI systems act in such settings, their behavior cannot be evaluated only by accuracy, loss, or average reward against a fixed distribution. The relevant question becomes strategic: how does the system behave when others adapt to it, exploit it, imitate it, or organize around it?

This observation creates a natural reason to revisit the relation between AI and game theory. The connection is often stated in instrumental terms: AI can help solve games, and game theory can help analyze AI. That description is correct but too weak. The deeper connection is that the two fields now meet at a shared bottleneck. Game theory supplies precise strategic specifications, but those specifications are often difficult to construct, infer, or implement at scale. AI supplies scalable procedures for search, function approximation, simulation, and language-based generation, but those procedures often lack a principled account of what their interactive behavior should satisfy. The central gap is therefore not simply between two literatures. It is between strategic conditions defined over counterfactual interaction and computational procedures that must learn, approximate, or verify those conditions from finite data and finite computation.

This is also the gap in the existing conversation. Work at the interface of AI and game theory is abundant, but it is usually organized by local technical communities: equilibrium computation, multi-agent reinforcement learning, empirical game-theoretic analysis, neural mechanism design, behavioral modeling, AI alignment, or algorithmic governance. Each community has developed its own methods and benchmarks, yet the common problem is often left implicit. Equilibrium solvers, inverse-game methods, neural auctions, LLM evaluations, self-play alignment procedures, and regret-based audits all ask how a counterfactual strategic property can be represented inside a computational system. A positioning survey should therefore not merely list applications of AI to games or applications of games to AI. It should explain why these applications belong to the same intellectual movement.

The gap appears first on the game-theory side. Equilibrium is defined by the absence of profitable deviations, but the deviations may range over enormous policy spaces. Strategic inference asks what latent values, beliefs, or social relations generated observed play, but the same behavior may be rationalized by many hidden games. Mechanism design asks for rules under which truthful behavior is optimal for every type and every misreport, but that universal incentive constraint becomes hard to maintain in high-dimensional, combinatorial, or continuous environments. In each case, the difficulty is not that the theoretical object is vague. The difficulty is that it is too explicit: it quantifies over counterfactual possibilities that are too large to enumerate directly.

AI changes what can be done with these objects. It can parameterize strategies and mechanisms, approximate value and regret functions, search for deviations, simulate populations, invert structured behavior maps, and generate candidate symbolic rules. But AI does not remove the game-theoretic constraint. Instead, it relocates it. In equilibrium computation, the Nash certificate may become a differentiable residual, a no-regret trajectory, an empirical-game approximation, an online re-solving guarantee, or a symbolic proof. In strategic inference, behavioral theories become trainable generators that can be constructed, inverted, and expanded. In mechanism design, incentive compatibility moves from an external constraint to a regret penalty, then into architectures, truthful representations, verification procedures, and eventually interpretable institutional code. This is the first direction of the survey: \emph{AI for game theory}. AI is valuable here not because it replaces strategic reasoning with black-box prediction, but because it helps make strategic specifications executable.

The same gap also appears from the AI side, but in reverse. Once AI systems themselves become strategic actors, ordinary machine-learning evaluation is under-specified. A model may score well against a benchmark yet fail against adaptive opponents. A policy may maximize a reward signal yet induce undesirable equilibria when deployed with other learning agents. A locally aligned agent may still contribute to collusion, manipulation, delegation failure, or systemic welfare loss when embedded in markets and institutions. These are not merely engineering failures; they are failures to specify the relevant interactive object. The unit of analysis is no longer an isolated model, but a policy in relation to other policies, users, protocols, and institutional rules.

Game theory supplies the missing vocabulary for this side of the problem. It asks whether competence should be measured by win rate, exploitability, population ranking, or equilibrium robustness. It reframes alignment not only as reward maximization, but as equilibrium selection in adversarial games, preference games, and interactive-proof games. It treats deployment governance not as source-code inspection alone, but as the auditing of behavioral properties such as regret, calibrated regret, equilibrium selection, and system-level welfare. This is the second direction of the survey: \emph{game theory for AI}. Game theory is valuable here not because it gives a single normative solution for all AI systems, but because it identifies which relational properties must be evaluated, optimized, and governed when AI systems interact strategically.

The organizing thesis of this survey is therefore that AI and game theory are converging around the problem of making strategic interaction operational. From one direction, AI helps game theory move from existence, identification, and incentive constraints toward computable artifacts. From the other direction, game theory helps AI move from scalar performance toward relational robustness. The bridge between the two is counterfactual structure: deviations not taken, types not observed, opponents not yet encountered, preferences not reducible to a scalar, and institutions whose effects emerge only after agents adapt. A useful AI--game theory interface must therefore do more than fit observed behavior or compute a point solution. It must connect procedures to the strategic conditions those procedures are meant to satisfy.

For this reason, the survey is organized by problems rather than by techniques. The guiding questions are not "Which AI method has been applied to games?" or "Which game-theoretic concept has been imported into AI?" They are instead: when an equilibrium is specified, how can it be constructed or certified; when behavior is observed, what latent strategic state has been identified; when a mechanism is learned, where does incentive compatibility live; when an AI system acts strategically, how should competence be evaluated; when alignment is interactive, what equilibrium is selected; and when many aligned agents are deployed together, what system-level outcome do they induce?

The remainder of the paper develops this thesis in two parts. Section~2 reviews the game-theoretic concepts and AI methods needed for the analysis. Section~3 studies \emph{AI for game theory}. Section~3.1 examines constructive equilibrium methods, asking how AI helps search for, relax, approximate, or re-certify Nash-like solution concepts. Section~3.2 turns to strategic inference, asking how behavioral theories and game structures can become trainable generators for hidden strategic states. Section~3.3 studies neural mechanism design, asking where incentive compatibility should live inside a learned mechanism: in the loss, the architecture, the representation, the verifier, or the generated institutional rule. Section~4 then studies \emph{game theory for AI}. Section~4.1 examines strategic evaluation, where competence is opponent-relative and must be assessed through population and equilibrium-based notions. Section~4.2 examines training and tuning, where alignment problems are recast as equilibrium-selection problems. Section~4.3 examines deployment governance, where locally aligned agents can induce system-level equilibria that require behavioral audit and institutional control. Section~5 concludes by discussing the resulting co-evolution: more capable strategic AI systems demand richer game-theoretic specifications, while richer specifications enable the construction of more capable and more governable AI systems.
