\cmt[step]{(1) Synthesize. Restate the shared problem, then what each direction
did to it. Reuse Part~I's recurring-move sentence and Part~II's scalar$\to$object
spine verbatim in substance.}
The six domains answer one question from two sides: how can a strategic property
defined over counterfactual interaction be represented inside a computational
system? Section~\ref{sec:ai4gt} takes the property as given and makes it
constructible. Across constructive equilibrium, strategic inference, and neural
mechanism design, the recurring move relocates a game-theoretic constraint into a
computational substrate (a differentiable residual, a no-regret trajectory, an
architectural bias, or a checkable certificate), while the guarantee itself
re-enters through a classical layer such as linear programming, no-regret theory,
safe resolving, or symbolic proof. Section~\ref{sec:gt4ai} takes the construction
as given and asks what it must satisfy. Across strategic evaluation, training, and
deployment governance, each step replaces a scalar with a game-theoretic object:
a benchmark score with exploitability, a fixed reward with the equilibrium of a
game, source-code inspection with an equilibrium-level audit.

\cmt[step]{(2) The common movement. The point is that it is the \emph{same}
object traveling both ways; that observation is the synthesis, not a restatement
of (1).}
A single object travels in both directions: the counterfactual certificate that
quantifies over deviations not taken, types not reported, and opponents not
encountered. Section~\ref{sec:ai4gt} carries it from specification toward
construction, building or approximating the certificate that classical theory
could only define. Section~\ref{sec:gt4ai} carries it the other way, attaching a
counterfactual specification to systems built before anyone asked what they
should satisfy. The specification--construction gap named in
Section~\ref{sec:intro} is therefore worked from both banks at once.

\cmt[step]{(3) Co-evolution. These two paragraphs are the tightened descendants
of the two co-evolution paragraphs cut from the intro (``Read together \dots''
and ``The two sides converge \dots''); the intro's \cmt[todo] points here. The
last sentence of the second paragraph is the intro's single forward-pointing
sentence, now developed.}
Read from both sides, the domains describe a co-evolution. On the first side,
strategic specifications become executable: a Nash equilibrium, an
incentive-compatible mechanism, or a correlated-equilibrium bound stops being
only an analytic object and becomes something a learning system can search for,
approximate, and certify. The game-theoretic constraint acquires a constructive
arm it lacked once the relevant games grew large.

On the second side, executions carry their own specifications. Once a trained
system negotiates, prices, or persuades, its competence becomes opponent-relative
and its alignment becomes a relation among policies, so the system itself
generates the demand for a strategic property to evaluate and govern.
Construction acquires a specification arm it lacked when behavior was scored
against a fixed test set. The two sides close into a loop: as strategic AI grows
more capable it demands richer specifications to stay legible and governable, and
richer specifications unlock the construction of still more capable systems.

\cmt[step]{(4) Research directions. User asked for a few possible directions, all
marked ``\emph{to be determined}.'' Each ties back to a tension the body already
exposed, so they are openings of this survey's own argument, not a generic
wish-list. Keep citations to keys already in ref.bib.}
Several open problems follow from the synthesis; the list below is a first pass,
and each item is flagged tentative pending the authors' final scoping.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Certified equilibrium beyond two-player zero-sum.} The complexity
        partition that organizes Section~\ref{subsec:constructive-equilibrium}
        (linear-programming tractability in zero-sum, PPAD-hardness in
        general-sum) still bounds what learned solvers can guarantee; methods that
        scale retreat to (C)CE and lose the exact certificate. A learned solver
        whose output carries a machine-checkable Nash guarantee in general-sum
        games would close the gap from the construction side.
  \item \textbf{An identifiability theory for neural behavior generators.}
        Section~\ref{subsec:strategic-inference} showed that fit is cheap and
        identification hard, with recovery guaranteed only under auxiliary
        assumptions such as entropy regularization. When the latent strategic
        state is recoverable from observed behavior, and relative to which assumed
        response map, remains open.
  \item \textbf{Exact incentive compatibility at deployment scale.}
        Section~\ref{subsec:mechanism-design} reached verifiable institutional
        artifacts, but exact strategy-proofness over multi-bidder, combinatorial,
        or continuous type spaces is enforced only
        partially~\cite{Duetting2024,WangBundleFlow2025}. Mechanisms that are
        truthful by construction and verified end-to-end at scale are still to be
        built.
  \item \textbf{Preference coherence as a precondition for alignment.}
        Section~\ref{subsec:strategic-evaluation} noted that exploitability and
        rankings presuppose a coherent utility whose existence is itself uncertain
        for current models~\cite{RossKimLo2024,Mazeika2025}. Auditing whether the
        utility exists and selecting the equilibrium it should play are treated
        separately by current pipelines.
  \item \textbf{Equilibrium-level governance standards.}
        Section~\ref{subsec:deployment-governance} framed calibrated-regret
        auditing as a recent program~\cite{HartlineWangZhang2025,Hartline2026}.
        Turning it into a deployable standard, with an agreed equilibrium-level
        property, test, and sample-complexity budget, is a direction for both
        theory and policy.
  \item \textbf{Language models as certificate engines.} LLMs appeared in
        Section~\ref{subsec:constructive-equilibrium} and
        Section~\ref{subsec:mechanism-design} as symbolic solvers and
        institutional compilers that restore the certificate in
        full~\cite{PrimeNash2025,LegoNE2025,LiuGuoConitzer2025}. Whether they can
        reliably emit machine-checkable strategic certificates, and under what
        verification, is unsettled.
\end{itemize}

\cmt[step]{(5) Close on the future of game theory itself, matching the intro's
promise (``\dots the future of game theory itself''). One paragraph; land on the
fourth turn of the field's binding question.}
Game theory has turned its binding question several times: from whether an
equilibrium exists, to how an institution should be designed, to what an
equilibrium costs to compute (Section~\ref{sec:recap}). The interface with AI adds
another turn. The objects of the theory, equilibria, incentive-compatible
mechanisms, and regret-bounded dynamics, are increasingly built, learned, and
audited inside computational systems, where earlier they were characterized on
paper. Game theory thereby gains a constructive and a governance face alongside
its analytic one, and the systems it governs gain a vocabulary precise enough to
stay accountable as they grow more capable.
