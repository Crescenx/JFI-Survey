\cmt[step]{(1) Current state. Game theory = a language for counterfactual
robustness; AI has turned interactive in exactly that sense. Land the move on:
competence is now a strategic question, not a scalar one.}
Game theory was built to reason about situations in which an outcome depends on
how multiple agents respond to one another. Its central concepts (Nash
equilibrium, Bayesian games, incentive compatibility, rationalizability, common
knowledge, mechanism design) are all ways of disciplining this dependence. Each
quantifies over what was \emph{not} observed: whether a proposed outcome would
survive deviations no one took, whether the beliefs that could have generated
the play are consistent, whether truthful reporting would remain optimal against
every possible misreport, whether an institution would still perform once
participants understand and adapt to its rules. Game theory is a language for
\emph{counterfactual robustness} in interactive systems.

That language has become newly urgent because artificial intelligence has turned
interactive in precisely this sense. Modern AI systems act in environments
shaped by other agents and by responses to their own behavior.
Reinforcement-learning agents learn through repeated feedback; self-play systems
reach superhuman strength in chess, Go, and poker by improving against changing
opponents~\cite{Silver2018,BrownSandholm2018}; language-model agents negotiate,
persuade, delegate, and coordinate; and pricing, recommendation, and trading
algorithms operate in environments shaped by other algorithms and by human
responses to them. Once a system acts in such a setting, its competence must be
measured strategically: how does it behave when others adapt to it, exploit it,
imitate it, or organize around it?

\cmt[step]{(2) The complementary deficit. GT specifies but cannot construct at
scale; AI constructs but cannot say what behavior should satisfy. Each lack is
the other's strength, so the two fields call for each other. Name the gap at the
END of this move, so step (3) has something concrete to turn on.}
AI and game theory meet at complementary deficits. Game theory supplies precise
strategic specifications, but once the number of agents, types, or states grows
large, it cannot \emph{construct} the procedures that reach them at scale. AI
supplies scalable procedures for search, function approximation, simulation, and
language-based generation, yet offers no principled account of what their
interactive behavior should \emph{satisfy}. Game theory tends toward
specification, AI toward construction. The gap between them runs between
strategic conditions defined over counterfactual interaction and computational
procedures that must learn, approximate, or verify those conditions from finite
data and finite computation.

\cmt[step]{(3) Why hard. Pivot on ``This gap is hard for a structural reason.''
Off-equilibrium counterfactuals -> combinatorial blow-up -> the object is too
explicit. Then the three obstructions (computational / epistemic / verification)
and the payoff line: closing the gap means restructuring the specification
$\leftrightarrow$ construction relation.}
This gap is hard for a structural reason. Every nontrivial strategic condition
is defined over \emph{off-equilibrium counterfactuals}: the deviations not
taken, the types not reported, the opponents not encountered, the private values
not observed. This counterfactual space grows combinatorially in the number of
agents, actions, and states, too vast to enumerate and too high-dimensional to
resolve in closed form. The theoretical object is too explicit; it quantifies
over more possibilities than any procedure can handle directly.

The obstruction takes three forms. When a condition is specified but its
construction is intractable, the obstruction is \emph{computational}: computing
a Nash equilibrium in general finite games is PPAD-complete, and the hardness
persists down to the two-player
case~\cite{DaskalakisGoldbergPapadimitriou2009,ChenDengTeng2009}. When a
procedure is observed but its governing condition is unknown, the obstruction is
\emph{epistemic}: the same behavior can be rationalized by many distinct payoff
structures, belief hierarchies, or reasoning rules, so conduct does not uniquely
reveal the private information that produced it. When the procedure is an opaque
neural system, the obstruction is one of \emph{verification}: properties such as
non-exploitability or incentive compatibility range over counterfactual
interactions that finite testing can never exhaust. Closing the gap therefore
means restructuring the relationship between a specification and its
construction.

\cmt[step]{(4) Bridging effort, organized by problem. The literature is
scattered across communities that share one implicit problem; organizing by
problem (not by community) reveals the common movement. This is where the
``problem-oriented'' choice of organization is justified by description, not
asserted. Then hand off to the two-direction breakdown, Part I / Part II, which
doubles as the article structure.}
Work at this interface is abundant, but it is scattered across communities
(equilibrium computation, multi-agent reinforcement learning, empirical
game-theoretic analysis, neural mechanism design, behavioral modeling, AI
alignment, algorithmic governance), each with its own methods and benchmarks and
each leaving a shared problem implicit. Equilibrium solvers, inverse-game
methods, neural auctions, language-model evaluations, self-play alignment
procedures, and regret-based audits all confront one question: how can a
counterfactual strategic property be represented inside a computational system?
Organizing this work by the problems it addresses, rather than by the community
that produced it, brings the common movement into view. We examine the gap from
both directions.

\paragraph{Part I: AI for game theory (Section 3).}
Here AI helps make strategic specifications executable. The key observation is
that AI relocates the game-theoretic constraint into a differentiable residual,
a no-regret trajectory, an architectural guarantee, or a checkable certificate.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Constructive equilibrium (3.1).} The Nash equilibrium is easy to
        define and hard to compute. We trace a spectrum of methods that relax,
        approximate, transform, or re-prove the equilibrium certificate, from
        differentiable residuals and no-regret self-play to language-model-based
        symbolic solvers. \emph{Given that the equilibrium certificate cannot be
        enforced globally, what detour around it does each method take?}
  \item \textbf{Strategic inference (3.2).} Observed behavior is consistent with
        many latent games. We examine how AI turns behavioral theories into
        trainable \emph{behavior generators} that can be constructed, inverted,
        and expanded, recovering hidden preferences, beliefs, and social
        relations. \emph{Given that fitting behavior is easy but identifying the
        mechanism is hard, what identification discipline does AI supply?}
  \item \textbf{Mechanism design (3.3).} Incentive compatibility requires
        truthful reporting to dominate every misreport, for every type and every
        opponent. We trace how this counterfactual constraint moves step by step
        inside the learning system: first as an external linear program, then as
        a differentiable regret penalty~\cite{Duetting2019}, then as an
        architectural guarantee, and finally as a verified institutional
        artifact. \emph{Where should incentive compatibility live in a neural
        mechanism?}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Part II: Game theory for AI (Section 4).}
Here game theory identifies the relational properties that must be evaluated,
optimized, and governed once AI systems interact. The unit of analysis becomes
a policy in relation to other policies, users, protocols, and institutions.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Strategic evaluation (4.1).} When performance depends on the
        opponent, a scalar benchmark score stops being a meaningful measure of
        competence. Game-theoretic notions such as exploitability,
        population-level rankings, and Nash averaging~\cite{Balduzzi2018}
        replace fixed test sets with opponent-relative evaluation. \emph{Can
        strategic competence be judged without specifying against whom?}
  \item \textbf{Training and alignment (4.2).} When the alignment target cannot
        be reduced to a fixed scalar reward, for instance when it involves an
        adversary, a non-transitive preference, or a verifier weaker than the
        model~\cite{Christiano2017,Ouyang2022}, training becomes equilibrium
        selection. We examine three game families: adversarial, preference, and
        interactive-proof games. \emph{When alignment is a relation among
        policies, what should training optimize?}
  \item \textbf{Deployment governance (4.3).} Individually aligned agents can
        induce undesirable system-level equilibria when they interact through
        markets, delegation chains, or pricing algorithms. No-regret learning
        theory and calibrated-regret auditing provide tools for governing
        strategic \emph{behavior} from the outside, without requiring inspection
        of source code. \emph{What system-level equilibria emerge from locally
        aligned agents, and how can they be audited?}
\end{itemize}

Read from both sides, these efforts point to a co-evolution that Section~5
develops in full: as strategic AI grows more capable it demands richer
specifications to remain legible and governable, and richer specifications in
turn unlock the construction of still more capable systems. The remainder of the
paper proceeds as follows. Section~2 reviews the game-theoretic concepts and AI
methods the analysis requires. Section~3 studies \emph{AI for game theory}
through constructive equilibrium (3.1), strategic inference (3.2), and neural
mechanism design (3.3). Section~4 studies \emph{game theory for AI} through
strategic evaluation (4.1), training and alignment (4.2), and deployment
governance (4.3). Section~5 concludes with what this co-evolution implies for
the design of AI systems, the governance of multi-agent environments, and the
future of game theory itself.
