\section{Introduction}

Game theory was built to reason about situations in which an outcome depends not
only on what one agent does, but on how others respond. Its central
concepts---Nash equilibrium, Bayesian games, incentive compatibility,
rationalizability, common knowledge, mechanism design---are all ways of
disciplining this dependence, and none of them is merely a description of
observed behavior. Each one quantifies over what was \emph{not} observed:
whether a proposed outcome would survive deviations no one took, whether the
beliefs that could have generated the play are consistent, whether truthful
reporting would remain optimal against every possible misreport, whether an
institution would still perform once participants understand and adapt to its
rules. In this sense game theory is a language for \emph{counterfactual
robustness} in interactive systems.

That language has become newly urgent because artificial intelligence has turned
interactive in precisely this sense. Modern AI systems are no longer only
predictors trained on fixed datasets. Reinforcement-learning agents learn
through repeated feedback; self-play systems reach superhuman strength in chess,
Go, and poker by improving against changing
opponents~\cite{Silver2018,BrownSandholm2018}; language-model agents negotiate,
persuade, delegate, and coordinate; and pricing, recommendation, and trading
algorithms increasingly operate in environments shaped by other algorithms and
by human responses to them. Once a system acts in such a setting, it can no
longer be judged by accuracy, loss, or average reward against a fixed
distribution. The relevant question becomes strategic: how does it behave when
others adapt to it, exploit it, imitate it, or organize around it?

This is a natural reason to revisit the relation between the two fields, but the
usual framing is too weak. It is often said that AI can help \emph{solve} games
and game theory can help \emph{analyze} AI. The deeper point is that the two
fields meet at complementary deficits. Game theory supplies precise strategic
specifications but, once the number of agents, types, or states grows large,
often cannot \emph{construct} the procedures that reach them at scale. AI
supplies scalable procedures for search, function approximation, simulation, and
language-based generation but offers no principled account of what their
interactive behavior ought to \emph{satisfy}. Stated as a slogan: game theory
tends toward specification without construction, AI toward construction without
specification. The slogan should not be read too literally---game theory does
construct, and AI objectives are a kind of specification---but the asymmetry is
real, and the gap it names is not between two literatures. It is between
strategic conditions defined over counterfactual interaction and computational
procedures that must learn, approximate, or verify those conditions from finite
data and finite computation.

\subsection{Why the gap is structurally hard}

The gap is not a matter of engineering convenience. It follows from a single
structural fact: every nontrivial strategic condition is defined over
\emph{off-equilibrium counterfactuals}---the deviations not taken, the types not
reported, the opponents not encountered, the private values not observed. This
counterfactual space grows combinatorially in the number of agents, actions, and
states, and is in general too vast to enumerate and too high-dimensional to
resolve in closed form. The difficulty is therefore not that the theoretical
object is vague; it is that the object is \emph{too explicit}, quantifying over
possibilities too numerous to handle directly.

The obstruction takes three forms. When a condition is given but its
construction is intractable, the obstruction is \emph{computational}: computing a
Nash equilibrium in general finite games is PPAD-complete, and the hardness
persists down to the two-player
case~\cite{DaskalakisGoldbergPapadimitriou2009,ChenDengTeng2009}. When a
procedure is given but its governing condition is unknown, the obstruction is
\emph{epistemic}: the same observed behavior can be rationalized by many distinct
payoff structures, belief hierarchies, or reasoning rules, so conduct does not
uniquely reveal the private information that produced it. When the procedure is
an opaque neural system, a further obstruction arises---\emph{verification}:
properties such as non-exploitability or incentive compatibility range over
counterfactual interactions that finite testing can never exhaust. The gap thus
cannot be closed by brute force from either side; it requires restructuring the
relationship between a specification and its construction.

\subsection{Why a positioning survey, and how it is organized}

Work at this interface is abundant, but it is usually organized by local
technical communities---equilibrium computation, multi-agent reinforcement
learning, empirical game-theoretic analysis, neural mechanism design, behavioral
modeling, AI alignment, algorithmic governance---each with its own methods and
benchmarks, and each leaving the common problem implicit. Equilibrium solvers,
inverse-game methods, neural auctions, language-model evaluations, self-play
alignment procedures, and regret-based audits all confront the same question:
how can a counterfactual strategic property be represented inside a computational
system? A useful survey should therefore not merely list applications of AI to
games or of games to AI. It should explain why these applications belong to the
same intellectual movement, and it should be organized by \emph{problems} rather
than by techniques. We examine the gap from both directions.

\paragraph{Part I: AI for game theory (Section 3).}
Here AI is valuable not because it replaces strategic reasoning with black-box
prediction, but because it helps make strategic specifications executable. The
key observation is that AI does not remove the game-theoretic constraint; it
\emph{relocates} it---into a differentiable residual, a no-regret trajectory, an
architectural guarantee, or a checkable certificate.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Constructive equilibrium (3.1).} The Nash equilibrium is easy to
        define and hard to compute. We trace a spectrum of methods that relax,
        approximate, transform, or re-prove the equilibrium certificate, from
        differentiable residuals and no-regret self-play to language-model-based
        symbolic solvers. \emph{Given that the equilibrium certificate cannot be
        enforced globally, what detour around it does each method take?}
  \item \textbf{Strategic inference (3.2).} Observed behavior is consistent with
        many latent games. We examine how AI turns behavioral theories into
        trainable \emph{behavior generators} that can be constructed, inverted,
        and expanded, recovering hidden preferences, beliefs, and social
        relations. \emph{Given that fitting behavior is easy but identifying the
        mechanism is hard, what identification discipline does AI supply?}
  \item \textbf{Mechanism design (3.3).} Incentive compatibility requires
        truthful reporting to dominate every misreport, for every type and every
        opponent. We trace how this counterfactual constraint is moved step by
        step inside the learning system: first as an external linear program,
        then as a differentiable regret penalty~\cite{Duetting2019}, then as an
        architectural guarantee, and finally as a verified institutional
        artifact. \emph{Where should incentive compatibility live in a neural
        mechanism?}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Part II: Game theory for AI (Section 4).}
Here game theory is valuable not because it dictates a single normative solution
for all AI systems, but because it identifies which relational properties must be
evaluated, optimized, and governed once AI systems interact. The unit of analysis
is no longer an isolated model but a policy in relation to other policies, users,
protocols, and institutions.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Strategic evaluation (4.1).} When performance depends on the
        opponent, a scalar benchmark score stops being a meaningful measure of
        competence. Game-theoretic notions---exploitability, population-level
        rankings, Nash averaging~\cite{Balduzzi2018}---replace fixed test sets
        with opponent-relative evaluation. \emph{Can strategic competence be
        judged without specifying against whom?}
  \item \textbf{Training and alignment (4.2).} When the alignment target cannot
        be reduced to a fixed scalar reward---because it involves an adversary, a
        non-transitive preference, or a verifier weaker than the
        model~\cite{Christiano2017,Ouyang2022}---training becomes equilibrium
        selection rather than optimization. We examine three game families
        (adversarial, preference, and interactive-proof games). \emph{When
        alignment is a relation rather than a score, what should training
        optimize?}
  \item \textbf{Deployment governance (4.3).} Individually aligned agents can
        induce undesirable system-level equilibria when they interact through
        markets, delegation chains, or pricing algorithms. No-regret learning
        theory and calibrated-regret auditing offer tools for governing strategic
        \emph{behavior} rather than inspecting source code. \emph{What
        system-level equilibria emerge from locally aligned agents, and how can
        they be audited?}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The deeper claim: co-evolution, not division of labor}

The six domains can be read as a division of labor---AI supplies the
computational engine, game theory the normative standard. Read together they
suggest something stronger. On the AI-for-game-theory side, specifications are
being reshaped to become executable: exact equilibrium conditions relax into
differentiable residuals or no-regret guarantees; incentive constraints migrate
into truthful-by-design architectures rather than after-the-fact penalties. On
the game-theory-for-AI side, executions are being equipped to carry their own
specifications: learned mechanisms ship with verifiable incentive guarantees,
solvers emit checkable certificates, and competence is measured through
exploitability rather than static accuracy.

This mutual adjustment is not two fields lending each other tools. It is a
\emph{convergence}: the constructed object and its governing condition are being
brought within one framework, joined by counterfactual structure---deviations not
taken, types not observed, opponents not yet encountered, preferences not
reducible to a scalar, institutions whose effects appear only after agents adapt.
If the trend continues, more capable strategic AI will demand richer
specifications to remain legible and governable, while richer specifications will
unlock the construction of still more capable systems.

The remainder of the paper develops this thesis. Section~2 reviews the
game-theoretic concepts and AI methods the analysis requires. Section~3 studies
\emph{AI for game theory} through constructive equilibrium (3.1), strategic
inference (3.2), and neural mechanism design (3.3). Section~4 studies \emph{game
theory for AI} through strategic evaluation (4.1), training and alignment (4.2),
and deployment governance (4.3). Section~5 concludes by taking up what this
co-evolution implies for the design of AI systems, the governance of multi-agent
environments, and the future of game theory itself.
